# Āthār al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb — Synopsis & Guide to Ibāḍī Musnads

## In-Depth Synopsis

### What This Work Is

The *Āthār al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb* is arguably the oldest surviving work devoted to Ibāḍī *fiqh* (jurisprudence). Edited and studied by Kahlan al-Kharusi — originally as a DPhil thesis at Oxford in 2003, later published by Harrassowitz Verlag in 2016 — it presents a critical edition of a text that predates, or is contemporary with, the earliest known works of Islamic legal literature from any school.

The work is not a "hadith collection" in the way Sunni readers might expect (i.e., organized thematically like al-Bukhārī's *Ṣaḥīḥ* or Mālik's *Muwaṭṭaʾ*). Instead, it is a raw transmission of legal opinions, prophetic traditions, and *fatāwā* from the early Ibāḍī scholarly circle in Basra, transmitted through a specific chain of authorities and preserved without later editorial rearrangement. This rawness is precisely what makes it invaluable: it is a photograph of pre-formative Islamic legal thinking, before the *madhāhib* had crystallized into their later institutional forms.

### The Transmission Chain

The primary *isnād* (chain of transmission) runs:

**Abū Ṣufra ʿAbd al-Malik b. Ṣufra** → **al-Haytham** → **al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb** → **Ḍumām b. al-Sāʾib** → **Jābir b. Zayd (Abū al-Shaʿthāʾ)**

Jābir b. Zayd (d. 93/711) is the central figure — a *tābiʿī* (Successor) from Firq, Oman who settled in Basra, where he studied under major Companions including Ibn ʿAbbās, ʿĀʾisha, and Anas b. Mālik. He is recognized by both Ibāḍī and Sunni traditions as a remarkable jurist. Aḥmad b. Ḥanbal described him as *thiqa* and *ṣadūq*, and al-Dhahabī called him *al-imām al-ḥujja*.

Al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb al-Farāhīdī (d. between 175-180/791-796) compiled these traditions, which were then transmitted by his students Abū Sufyān Maḥbūb and others. The work went through various hands until it reached the three manuscripts (Egyptian and two Tunisian) that form the basis of this critical edition.

### Content and Structure

The edited text contains **324 numbered traditions** (plus 2 interpolations marked E1 and E2), covering 25 legal-topical categories. The traditions are *not* organized thematically in the original text — they appear in the order they were transmitted. Al-Kharusi provides a comprehensive topic index in Chapter Three mapping each tradition to its *fiqh* category:

The largest categories are **Ḥajj and ʿUmra** (48 traditions), **al-Jināyāt / criminal law** (37 traditions), **Ṣalāt** (30 traditions), **Firāq / divorce** (30 traditions), and **Muʿāmalāt al-raqīq / dealings with slaves** (29 traditions). Marriage, inheritance, *zakāt*, fasting, food and drink, witnesses, oaths, manumission, and the treatment of non-Muslims are all represented. There are even 10 traditions dealing with political and historical events — including references to the assassination of ʿUthmān, the Battle of the Camel, and the revolt of Ibn al-Ashʿath.

### The Spirit of the Work

What makes the *Āthār* so remarkable is not merely its age, but what it reveals about the character of early Islamic legal discourse. Three qualities stand out:

**1. Intellectual independence.** Jābir b. Zayd does not simply pass on Prophetic traditions passively. He evaluates, weighs evidence, and sometimes holds opinions that his own students and later Ibāḍī authorities reject. For instance, his position on the marriage of minors (tradition [148]) was dismissed by later Ibāḍīs. His views on *tamattuʿ* in *ḥajj* (traditions [15], [136]) were considered strange even by contemporaries. This willingness to follow evidence wherever it leads, even into isolation, speaks to a legal culture where *ijtihād* (independent reasoning) was still vigorously alive.

**2. Cross-sectarian openness.** The *Āthār* displays a striking feature: early Ibāḍī scholars had no strict reservations about referring to authorities outside their community. Jābir was questioned by non-Ibāḍīs, his opinions were transmitted by non-Ibāḍīs, and his students openly engaged with the broader intellectual landscape of Basra. The work contains opinions from figures claimed by multiple traditions — ʿAlī, Ibn ʿAbbās, ʿĀʾisha, Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī, ʿAṭāʾ, and the "Kūfiyyūn" (Kufan scholars). This is a mutual relation that is rarely observable in later Muslim times, as al-Kharusi notes.

**3. A window into daily life.** Beyond abstract legal questions, the *Āthār* preserves the texture of 1st-2nd century AH society in Basra and the Ḥijāz — what people wore, how they prayed on the Ka'ba, disputes over irrigation rights, the clothing of *iḥrām*, the treatment of slaves and freed persons, questions about animals and food, the handling of menstruation and ritual purity. It is social anthropology disguised as *fiqh*.

### Significance for Islamic Scholarship

Al-Kharusi's study argues convincingly that the development of Ibāḍī *fiqh* is closer to the time of the Prophet and his Companions than that of the Sunni schools (which cannot therefore be normative for the Ibāḍī tradition). The *Āthār* provides evidence that the Ibāḍī legal system was growing and prevailing at a pre-formative stage of Islamic law — before the canonical schools had solidified.

This has profound implications for anyone studying the formation of Muslim schools of law: it means the Ibāḍī tradition is not a derivative "sect" that broke away from a pre-existing orthodoxy, but rather one of several parallel developments from the same common source of early Islamic practice.

### The Three Manuscripts

The edition is based on three manuscripts:

- **E (Egyptian)**: Preserved at Dār al-Kutub al-Miṣriyya (B/21582). Very small Maghribī hand, 163 folios, badly deteriorated. Contains the *Āthār* from folio 73/i to 80/i. Colophon dates to Ramaḍān 41/661 (likely actually 1041/1631).

- **T1 (First Tunisian)**: Used by Ennami in his edition of *Ajwibat Ibn Khalfūn*. Copied by Ṣāliḥ b. Sālim b. Sulaymān b. Yadar al-Sadrātī, completed Monday 23 Shawwāl 1191 AH (24 November 1777).

- **T2 (Second Tunisian)**: A photocopy from a private collection. Contains only 33 pages of the *Āthār* but provides important variant readings.

### The 22 Transmitting Authorities

Al-Kharusi identifies and provides biographical entries for 22 scholars who transmitted material in the *Āthār*, along with a tentative chronology placing them from the late 1st century to the early 3rd century AH. This prosopographical data is invaluable for understanding the Ibāḍī scholarly network in Basra.

## Where to Find the Official Musnad

The work you've uploaded — *Āthār al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb* (al-Kharusi's critical edition) — is the **original, unredacted text** as al-Rabīʿ compiled it. However, most Ibāḍīs know a different version of this collection: the **rearranged and expanded version** called *al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ — Musnad al-Imām al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb*, which was reorganized by **Abū Yaʿqūb Yūsuf b. Ibrāhīm al-Warjilānī** (d. 570/1174) and later annotated by **Nūr al-Dīn al-Sālimī** (d. 1332/1914).

Here is how the two relate and where to find the more "official" complete musnad:

### 1. *Tartīb al-Musnad* / *al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ* (al-Warjilānī's Rearrangement)

This is the standard Ibāḍī hadith collection containing approximately **1,005 hadiths** arranged thematically in the style of classical *fiqh* works. Al-Warjilānī took the original material of al-Rabīʿ and reorganized it, adding further narrations to create a comprehensive collection. This is the version that Ibāḍīs consider the most authoritative hadith work after the Qurʾān.

**Where to find it:**
- **Omani Ministry of Heritage & Culture editions** — Multiple printings from Muscat, available in Omani bookshops and through the Ministry
- **Maktabat Masqaṭ** — The al-Sālimī edition (*Sharḥ al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ Musnad al-Imām al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb*) is available through Omani publishers
- **Dār al-Fatḥ, Beirut** — Various Arabic editions
- Online: PDF versions are available through Ibāḍī digital libraries and ResearchGate

### 2. *Sharḥ al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ* (al-Sālimī's Commentary)

Nūr al-Dīn ʿAbd Allāh b. Ḥumayd al-Sālimī (d. 1332/1914), the great Omani scholar, wrote a comprehensive commentary on the Musnad. This is the most important scholarly apparatus for understanding the hadiths.

**Where to find it:**
- Published by the Omani Ministry of National Heritage (multiple volumes)
- Available in major Islamic libraries

### 3. English Translation

An English translation from French exists: *Jami Sahih: The Musnad of al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb* — available on Scribd and through some Ibāḍī publishers. However, it is translated from a French intermediary and should be used with caution for scholarly purposes.

### 4. Academic Studies

For scholarly engagement with the Ibāḍī hadith tradition:

- **Al-Qannūbī**, Saʿīd b. Mabrūk: *al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb: makānatuh wa musnaduh* (Oman 1995) — the most comprehensive Omani traditionalist study
- **Al-Rāshidī**, Mubārak: *al-Imām Abū ʿUbayda Muslim b. Abī Karīma al-Tamīmī wa fiqhuh* (Oman 1993) — nearly 700 pages
- **Ennami**, ʿAmr Khalīfa: *Studies in Ibāḍism* (1971) — the foundational Western academic study
- **Crone & Zimmermann**: *The Epistle of Sālim b. Dhakwān* (Oxford 2001) — essential for early Ibāḍī history
- **Francesca, E.**: *La fabbricazione degli Isnād nella Scuola ibāḍita: il Musnad ar-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb* — critical study of the isnād construction

### 5. Digital Resources

- **Ibadica.org** — A scholarly bibliography project with references to Ibāḍī works
- **Bint Ibadh** (bintibadh.blogspot.com) — Blog with English-language resources on Ibāḍī hadith
- **Ahlulistiqamah.co.uk** — Ibāḍī resource site with information on *al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ*

### Key Distinction: Āthār vs. Musnad

It's important to understand the relationship between what you have and the "official" version:

| Feature | Āthār al-Rabīʿ (this PDF) | al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaḥīḥ / Musnad |
|---------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Compiler | al-Rabīʿ b. Ḥabīb (d. ~175-180 AH) | Rearranged by al-Warjilānī (d. 570 AH) |
| Structure | Unorganized (original transmission order) | Thematically arranged by *fiqh* topics |
| Content | 324 traditions (original core) | ~1,005 hadiths (expanded with additional narrations) |
| Manuscripts | 3 manuscripts (Egyptian + 2 Tunisian) | Many copies, widely printed |
| Edition | Critical academic edition (al-Kharusi, 2016) | Multiple popular editions |
| Status | Academic/scholarly | The standard Ibāḍī hadith reference |

The *Āthār* is the scholarly-critical recovery of the **original kernel**; the *Musnad/Jāmiʿ* is the **living tradition** as practiced and taught in Ibāḍī communities. Both are valuable for different purposes.
